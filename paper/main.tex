\begin{abstract}
Discrete preference diffusion recommenders such as PreferGrow corrupt a
user's preferred item by fading it toward a \emph{proposal distribution}
and learn to grow the preference back. Existing personalization in this
family acts entirely on the \emph{score} side---the transition kernel
itself is shared by all users. We personalize the kernel: an explicit,
history-only text-evidence reliability signal gates a convex interpolation
between the host's learned global proposal and a frozen text-content
anchor, so that a single user-conditioned proposal jointly shapes where
preference fades and where it grows back. The design is
\emph{fallback-safe}: the reliability signal is calibrated against a
length-matched random-history null of the evidence space, the gate is zero
at or below the null, and we prove that the resulting chain (i) retains
Markovianity, detailed balance, and the closed-form analytic reversal of
the host kernel, (ii) reduces \emph{exactly} to the host kernel when the
gate closes, and (iii) deviates from it by at most the gate value in total
variation. This turns dataset-level differences in evidence quality into a
falsifiable prediction: gains where text evidence is informative, parity
where it is not. Across four sequential-recommendation benchmarks the
prediction is confirmed: \pending{one-sentence main-table summary from
Gate-2 report; strong exit: gains on Steam/ATG/Beauty and parity on ML1M
(delta test NDCG@10 $=$ \pnum)}. Controls (reliability shuffling, global
proposals, ungated anchors) and text-corruption diagnostics show the gains
require an \emph{aligned} per-user signal, and that the kernel backs off
adaptively as evidence degrades.

\section{Introduction}

Diffusion-based recommenders replace the single-step prediction of a
sequential recommender with an iterative corrupt-and-reconstruct process.
The discrete instantiation of this idea---preference fading and growing
over the item vocabulary~\cite{prefergrow}---is attractive because the
corruption process is defined directly on the discrete object the system
ranks: the forward kernel fades the preferred item into a proposal
distribution over alternatives, and the reverse process grows the
preference back under a learned score model.

The proposal distribution is where this family encodes ``what replaces a
faded preference.'' It determines the stationary distribution of the
forward chain, the reference measure of its detailed balance, the weights
of the score-entropy objective, and the analytic reverse ratio. Yet in
existing discrete preference diffusion the proposal is \emph{global}: a
single distribution---uniform, popularity, or learned---shared by every
user. Personalization enters only through the score network and through
inference-time guidance against a ``non-preference user''
branch~\cite{prefergrow}, i.e., entirely on the score side.
% One-line positioning (keep verbatim somewhere in intro):
% PreferGrow personalizes the score; we personalize the kernel --- safely.

We ask the complementary question: \emph{can the kernel itself be
personalized---safely?} Side information such as item text suggests an
answer: when a user's history is textually coherent, content similarity
identifies plausible substitutes, and fading preference toward
content-similar items should sharpen the learning signal. But side
evidence is not uniformly trustworthy---across users \emph{and across
datasets}. A movie catalog whose ``text'' is a title and a genre tag
carries far less evidence than a product catalog with rich descriptions.
A kernel that tilts toward text on every dataset will be at the mercy of
the evidence quality it happens to receive; we show this failure mode is
real and measurable.

Our design principle is \emph{fallback-safe evidence conditioning}. An
explicit, history-only reliability signal $\ut$ measures how far the
user's behavior-text coherence exceeds what a \emph{random} history would
score in the same evidence space (a length-matched null). A monotone gate
$g(\ut)\in[0,g_{\max}]$, zero at or below the null, interpolates between
the host's \emph{learned} global proposal and a frozen text anchor. The
pseudo-item mass of the host kernel is left untouched. Conditional on the
(fixed, history-derived) signal, all structural properties of the host
chain survive; when the gate closes, the kernel \emph{is} the host kernel,
exactly; and at any gate value the kernel is within $g(\ut)$ of the host
in total variation. The theory is deliberately elementary---rank-one
kernel algebra---but it buys a property that score-side or loss-side
side-information injection cannot even express: a provable reduction to
the host when evidence is uninformative.

This yields a falsifiable prediction rather than a uniform-superiority
claim. On datasets whose text evidence is informative (real histories
score above the null), the gated kernel should help; on datasets whose
text bank is statistically indistinguishable from its own null, the gate
should close and performance should match the host. Our experiments
across Steam, ML1M, Amazon Beauty, and ATG are designed around this
prediction, with reliability-shuffle and ungated-anchor controls that
separate \emph{aligned} per-user reliability from generic proposal biases.

Our contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Kernel-side personalization.} The first discrete
  preference-diffusion recommender whose transition kernel is conditioned
  per user by an explicit, history-only evidence-reliability signal;
  conditional Markovianity, the stationary proposal, detailed balance,
  and the analytic reverse ratio of the host all carry over
  (Props.~\ref{prop:validity}--\ref{prop:reverse}).
  \item \textbf{Fallback safety.} An exact-reduction guarantee
  (Prop.~\ref{prop:reduction}) and a total-variation bound
  (Lemma~\ref{lem:tv}), enabled by anchoring the interpolation to the
  host's learned proposal and freezing the pseudo-item mass; plus a
  null-calibration procedure showing that \emph{uncalibrated} reliability
  signals inherit dataset-level embedding geometry and produce spurious,
  regime-dependent behavior.
  \item \textbf{Mechanism evidence.} Controls (reliability shuffle,
  global proposal, ungated anchor), corruption diagnostics, and
  calibration bucketing that show the kernel genuinely uses aligned
  reliability to reshape its proposal and to back off under degraded
  evidence.
  \item \textbf{A confirmed falsifiable prediction.} Across four
  benchmarks, gains track measured evidence informativeness:
  \pending{fill from Gate-2 report (strong exit: positive on

\end{itemize}

\section{Preliminaries: Discrete Preference Diffusion}
\label{sec:prelim}

Let $\Omega=\{1,\dots,M\}$ be the item vocabulary; following
PreferGrow~\cite{prefergrow}, an optional non-preference pseudo-item is
appended as the last coordinate, giving $|\Omega|=M{+}1$ states. We use
the row-vector convention: a distribution $\mu$ evolves as $\mu P$.

Given a proposal distribution $p$ on $\Omega$, define the rank-one kernel
$\Pi_p(i,j)=p(j)$ and the generator $Q_p=\Pi_p-I$. With noise scale
$\sigma\ge 0$ and $\alpha_\sigma=e^{-\sigma}$, the forward transition is
\begin{equation}
  P_{\sigma,p} \;=\; e^{\sigma Q_p} \;=\; \alpha_\sigma I +
  (1-\alpha_\sigma)\,\Pi_p ,
  \label{eq:forward}
\end{equation}
so a preferred item survives with probability $\alpha_\sigma$ and is
otherwise resampled from $p$: preference \emph{fades toward the
proposal}. The score model $s_\Theta(x_t,t,u)$ (with $u$ a SASRec-style
history encoding) is trained by denoising score entropy~\cite{sedd} and
the reverse process uses the analytic stationary-reversal ratio in which
the proposal appears in closed form (Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}). The host
model learns its proposal: $\pcore=\mathrm{softmax}(p_1)$ with $p_1$ a
free parameter vector, trained jointly. All of
Sec.~\ref{sec:method} treats the host score model, objective, and
schedule as fixed---we modify \emph{only} the proposal.

\section{Method: Fallback-Safe Gated Proposals}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Evidence anchor}
Each dataset has a frozen text bank: one text record per item (title,
category, description where present), embedded once by an off-the-shelf
text encoder and never fine-tuned. For a user history
$h=(i_1,\dots,i_L)$ let $\bar e_h$ be the normalized mean of its item
embeddings and define the \emph{content anchor}
\begin{equation}
  \ch(i) \;\propto\; \exp\!\big(\langle \bar e_h, e_i\rangle/\tau_0\big),
  \label{eq:anchor}
\end{equation}
a distribution over real items, down-weighted for items with missing
text. The anchor proposes \emph{content-plausible substitutes}; it never
replaces the host's score representation.

\subsection{Null-calibrated reliability}
\label{sec:null}
The raw coherence statistic
$A_h=\frac{1}{L}\sum_{\ell}\tfrac{1}{2}\big(\langle e_{i_\ell},\bar
e_h\rangle+1\big)$ has a high and \emph{dataset-dependent} floor: a
random pseudo-history already scores
\pending{production-bank medians from Gate-0 report; legacy-bank
indication: $\approx0.81$ (ML1M), $0.77$ (Beauty), $0.87$ (Steam)}
because the statistic inherits the global geometry of the embedding
space. Consuming $A_h$ in absolute terms therefore couples the kernel's
operating point to arbitrary dataset geometry---the source of spurious
``regime-dependent'' behavior we document in Sec.~\ref{sec:calib}.

We calibrate against the evidence-space null. For each dataset we draw
random pseudo-histories of each observed length $L$ from the frozen bank
and record $\mu_{\mathrm{null}}(L)$, $\sigma_{\mathrm{null}}(L)$; the
\emph{calibrated reliability} of a real history is
\begin{equation}
  \ut \;=\; \frac{A_h-\mu_{\mathrm{null}}(L)}{k\,\sigma_{\mathrm{null}}(L)},
  \qquad
  g(\ut) \;=\; g_{\max}\cdot \mathrm{clip}(\ut,0,1).
  \label{eq:gate}
\end{equation}
$\ut$ is history-only (no target, no diffusion state, no current model
score) and fixed along the whole forward/reverse trajectory, as required
by the theory. By construction $g=0$ for histories at or below the null:
\emph{a dataset whose text bank carries no signal closes its own gate}.

\subsection{The gated kernel}
Let $\pcore=\mathrm{softmax}(p_1)$ be the host's learned proposal
(including the pseudo-item coordinate) and $\pcorer$ its restriction to
real items, renormalized. The user-conditioned proposal is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \pu \;=\;& \pcore(\star)\, e_\star \\
  &+ \big(1-\pcore(\star)\big)\Big[(1-g(\ut))\,\pcorer +
  g(\ut)\,\ch\Big],
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:pu}
\end{equation}
where $\star$ is the pseudo-item ($\pu=(1-g)\pcore+g\,\ch$ when no
pseudo-item is used). Three deliberate restrictions:
(i) the \emph{pseudo-item mass is exactly the host's} and the gate never
touches it; (ii) $g$ is the \emph{only} dial---no reliability-dependent
temperature or popularity mixing; (iii) no new learnable modules: the
only trained parameter in the proposal path is the host's own $p_1$,
shared with the fallback branch. The proposal $\pu$ replaces the global
proposal in every kernel-level object: forward corruption
\eqref{eq:forward}, score-entropy weights, and the analytic reverse
ratio (all call sites, not just negative sampling).

\subsection{Theory}
\label{sec:theory}
Proofs are in Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}. Fix a user history $h$, hence
$\ut$ and $\pu$, for the whole trajectory.

\begin{proposition}[Kernel validity; informal summary of
Props.~\ref{prop:forward-app}--\ref{prop:reverse-app}]
\label{prop:validity}
With $\pu$ in place of the global proposal, the forward corruption has
the closed form
$q_t(x_t{=}x \mid x_0,u)=\bar\alpha_t\mathbf{1}\{x{=}x_0\}
+(1-\bar\alpha_t)\pu(x)$; conditional on $\ut$ the chain is Markov; its
stationary and limiting distribution is $\pu$; it satisfies detailed
balance w.r.t.\ $\pu$; and the analytic stationary-reversal ratio is the
host's formula with the proposal term replaced by $\pu$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proposition}[Exact reduction / fallback safety]
\label{prop:reduction}
If $g(\ut)=0$ then $\pu=\pcore$, hence $Q_{\pu}=Q_{\pcore}$ and every
derived object---forward corruption, stationary distribution,
score-entropy weights, analytic reverse ratio---coincides exactly with
the host kernel's.
\end{proposition}
\begin{lemma}[Bounded deviation]
\label{lem:tv}
$\TV(\pu,\pcore)\le g(\ut)$, and each row of the forward transition
satisfies
$\TV\big(P_{\sigma,\pu}(i,\cdot),P_{\sigma,\pcore}(i,\cdot)\big)\le
(1-\alpha_\sigma)\,g(\ut)$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{remark}[Why the kernel is the right injection site]
\label{rem:site}
Encoder- or loss-level injection of the same signal admits no analogue
of Prop.~\ref{prop:reduction}: there is no sense in which ``the encoder
with text features at reliability zero'' \emph{is} the host model. The
reduction guarantee is expressible only where the conditioning enters
the transition law itself. Our injection-site comparison
(Sec.~\ref{sec:threeway}) is therefore an analysis of regimes, not the
basis of the safety claim.
\end{remark}
The guarantee has teeth only under the stated scope: $\ut$ history-only
and frozen per trajectory, self-resampling corruption, and the
pseudo-item coordinate present in every kernel object
(Appendix~\ref{app:boundary} lists the failure modes otherwise).

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Diffusion recommenders.}
Continuous-diffusion recommenders inject Gaussian noise in a latent or
embedding space~\cite{dreamrec,diffurec,preferdiff}; discrete
formulations operate on the item vocabulary
directly~\cite{prefergrow,llada-rec}. Our host is
PreferGrow~\cite{prefergrow}, whose personalization is score-side
(condition dropout plus inference-time guidance against a
non-preference-user branch); its kernel is user-independent. We supply
the complementary, kernel-side half, anchored to their learned proposal.

\paragraph{Shaped transition kernels in discrete diffusion.}
Structured (non-uniform) corruption matrices date to
D3PM~\cite{d3pm}; in recommendation, CDRec~\cite{cdrec} shapes
transition matrices with item popularity and TDPM~\cite{tdpm} modulates
token diffusion with temporal preference structure. All are global or
item-level signals; none condition the kernel per user on an explicit
evidence-reliability signal, and none provide a reduction guarantee to a
host kernel.

\paragraph{Guidance and adaptivity.}
Classifier-free guidance~\cite{cfg} and its recommendation
uses~\cite{cfgrec} interpolate conditional and unconditional
\emph{scores} with a global weight; adaptive guidance based on model
confidence exists for language diffusion~\cite{acfg}. Our gate is the
kernel-side analogue with the weight determined by measurable,
history-only evidence reliability---and with the interpolation target
being the host's learned proposal, which is what makes the safety
property provable.

\paragraph{Reliability and side information in recommendation.}
Reliability-aware recommenders act at the representation, optimization,
or decision level~\cite{ugr,truthsr}; text-side sequential recommenders
fuse content into encoders~\cite{recformer,unisrec}. Neither line
conditions a diffusion transition law; our text anchor is an
instantiation of evidence, not the contribution itself.

\section{Conclusion, Limitations}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We personalized the transition kernel of discrete preference diffusion
with an explicit, null-calibrated, history-only evidence-reliability
gate, anchored to the host's learned proposal so that the conditioning
is fallback-safe: exact reduction when the gate closes, bounded
deviation otherwise, and full inheritance of the host chain's structure.
Empirically the design converts evidence quality from a hidden
confounder into a falsifiable prediction: \pending{one-sentence frozen
Gate-2 summary}.

\emph{Limitations.} Gains are bounded by how much signal the evidence
space actually carries---by design, a closed gate can only match the
host, never beat it; the reliability signal is a single calibrated
statistic and richer signals (and their calibration) are open; our
metadata-sparsity diagnostic did not support the stronger hypothesis
that sparsity bucketing alone predicts per-user gains
(Appendix~\ref{app:results} reports this negative result); and the
guarantee is specific to kernels with a stationary proposal---schedule-
or mask-based discrete diffusions need their own analysis.

\section{Proofs}
\label{app:proofs}

Throughout, fix the user history $h$, the calibrated signal $\ut$, the
gate $g=g(\ut)$, and hence the proposal $\pu$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:pu};
$\pu$ is strictly positive whenever $\pcore$ and (on its support)
$\ch$ are, which holds by softmax parameterization with the completeness
down-weighting absorbed into logits. Row-vector convention; $\alpha_\sigma=e^{-\sigma}$.

\begin{proposition}[Proposal-conditioned forward corruption]
\label{prop:forward-app}
Let $\Pi_u(i,j)=\pu(j)$ and $Q_u=\Pi_u-I$. Then
$P_{\sigma,u}=e^{\sigma Q_u}=\alpha_\sigma I+(1-\alpha_\sigma)\Pi_u$,
and for discrete steps with $\bar\alpha_t=\prod_{r\le t}\alpha_r$,
\[
  q_t(x_t{=}x\mid x_0,u)=\bar\alpha_t\,\mathbf{1}\{x{=}x_0\}
  +(1-\bar\alpha_t)\,\pu(x).
\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$\Pi_u$ is an idempotent projection: $\Pi_u^2(i,j)=\sum_k
\pu(k)\pu(j)=\pu(j)=\Pi_u(i,j)$. Since $\Pi_u$ and $I$ commute,
$e^{\sigma Q_u}=e^{-\sigma}e^{\sigma\Pi_u}
=e^{-\sigma}\big(I+(e^{\sigma}-1)\Pi_u\big)
=\alpha_\sigma I+(1-\alpha_\sigma)\Pi_u$.
Products telescope by idempotence:
$\prod_t\big(\alpha_t I+(1-\alpha_t)\Pi_u\big)
=\bar\alpha_t I+(1-\bar\alpha_t)\Pi_u$; take row $x_0$, column $x$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Conditional Markovianity]
\label{prop:markov-app}
Conditional on the fixed $\ut$, $(x_t)$ generated by
$P_{\Delta\sigma_t,u}$ is a (time-inhomogeneous, per-step-size) Markov
chain; under a fixed step size it is generated by the single generator
$Q_u$, hence a homogeneous Markov semigroup.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
With $\ut$ fixed, $\pu$ and $Q_u$ are deterministic; each transition
reads only $(x_t,\Delta\sigma_t)$, never earlier states, so the
conditional law of $x_{t+1}$ given the past equals
$P_{\Delta\sigma_t,u}(x_t,\cdot)$. Without conditioning on $\ut$, the
mixture over users need not be a single Markov chain.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Stationary and limiting proposal]
\label{prop:stationary-app}
$\pu P_{\sigma,u}=\pu$ for all $\sigma\ge0$, and for any initial law
$\mu$, $\mu P_{\sigma,u}=\alpha_\sigma\mu+(1-\alpha_\sigma)\pu\to\pu$
as $\sigma\to\infty$. For finite $\sigma$ the marginal is the stated
mixture, not $\pu$ itself.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$\mu\Pi_u=\pu$ for any distribution $\mu$; substitute into
$P_{\sigma,u}=\alpha_\sigma I+(1-\alpha_\sigma)\Pi_u$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Detailed balance]
\label{prop:balance-app}
$\pu(i)P_{\sigma,u}(i,j)=\pu(j)P_{\sigma,u}(j,i)$ for all $i,j$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Diagonal terms are trivial; for $i\ne j$ both sides equal
$(1-\alpha_\sigma)\pu(i)\pu(j)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Analytic reverse ratio replaces only the proposal]
\label{prop:reverse-app}
For $\delta<\infty$, $P_{\delta,u}$ is invertible with
$P_{\delta,u}^{-1}=\alpha_\delta^{-1}I+(1-\alpha_\delta^{-1})\Pi_u$;
hence for any row vector $r$,
$rP_{\delta,u}^{-1}=e^{\delta}r+(1-e^{\delta})\langle
r,\mathbf{1}\rangle\,\pu$. The host's stationary-reversal operator is
recovered with $\pu$ in place of the global proposal; exact
non-stationary reverse kernels additionally depend on the current
marginal and remain the score model's job.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Multiply the two matrices and use $\Pi_u^2=\Pi_u$:
$[\alpha I+(1-\alpha)\Pi][\alpha^{-1}I+(1-\alpha^{-1})\Pi]=I$. The
row-vector form follows from $r\Pi_u=\langle r,\mathbf{1}\rangle\pu$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Exact reduction; Prop.~\ref{prop:reduction} in the
main text]
\label{prop:reduction-app}
If $g=0$ then $\pu=\pcore(\star)e_\star
+(1-\pcore(\star))\pcorer=\pcore$, hence $\Pi_u=\Pi_{\pcore}$,
$Q_u=Q_{\pcore}$, and every object in
Props.~\ref{prop:forward-app}--\ref{prop:reverse-app}, as well as the
score-entropy weights (which depend on the kernel only through $\pu$
and the schedule), coincides with the host's.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Immediate: $\pcorer$ renormalizes $\pcore$ off the pseudo-item, so
recombining with mass $\pcore(\star)$ restores $\pcore$ exactly; all
listed objects are functions of $(\pu,\text{schedule})$ alone.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[TV bound; Lemma~\ref{lem:tv} in the main text]
\label{lem:tv-app}
$\TV(\pu,\pcore)\le g$ and
$\TV\big(P_{\sigma,\pu}(i,\cdot),P_{\sigma,\pcore}(i,\cdot)\big)
\le(1-\alpha_\sigma)\,g$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
$\pu-\pcore=(1-\pcore(\star))\,g\,(\ch-\pcorer)$ on real coordinates
and $0$ on $\star$, so
$\TV(\pu,\pcore)=\tfrac12\|\pu-\pcore\|_1
=(1-\pcore(\star))\,g\,\TV(\ch,\pcorer)\le g$.
Rows of the transitions differ by $(1-\alpha_\sigma)(\pu-\pcore)$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Scope and failure modes}
\label{app:boundary}
The guarantees require: (1) $\ut=f(h)$ history-only---no dependence on
$x_t$, $t$, the target item, or current model scores; (2) $\ut$ fixed
along each forward/reverse trajectory (per-step updates give a Markov
chain on $(x_t,u_t)$ but not the same semigroup or balance structure);
(3) the pseudo-item coordinate present consistently in $\pu$ and every
kernel object; (4) self-resampling corruption---forced-replacement,
top-$k$ truncation, or state-dependent masking break detailed balance
and require new proofs; (5) hard-masked items restrict irreducibility
claims to the support; (6) the reverse-ratio statement concerns the
stationary-reversal analytic operator, not the exact non-stationary
reverse kernel; (7) if $g$ or $\ut$ leak batch labels at training time,
the certified object is a different, data-dependent kernel.
